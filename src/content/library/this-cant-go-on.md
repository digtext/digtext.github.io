## This Can't Go On
*This century sits at a hinge point in humanity's long growth story*

Holden Karnofsky · August 3, 2021 · [cold-takes.com](https://www.cold-takes.com/this-cant-go-on/#fnref1)


- For any timespan most of us can remember, worldwide output has expanded a few percent a year, and most people treat this Business As Usual headspace as a smooth extension of the past into the future.
    - Inside that headspace, change is noticeable but livable — there is a constant flow of new opportunities and new challenges, but if you want a few extra years to adapt to any given shift, you personally usually can.
        - 2019 looked pretty similar to 2018. 2010 was recognizably different but not alien. 1980 was another era, meaningfully but not crazily different from today.
            - Almost all mainstream news and analysis implicitly sits in this frame — assuming steady, predictable, manageable background growth is the landscape we will keep inhabiting.

- Zoom out past the last two centuries, though, and the picture inverts: growth has been accelerating for thousands of years, sits near an all-time peak right now, and cannot continue much longer — the This Can't Go On headspace.
    - The same historical data produces two very different charts depending on where you start. A chart beginning in 1950 shows a tidy diagonal. A chart beginning in 5000 BC shows a long flat line that goes near-vertical right around the present.
        - Business As Usual is a thin, recent slice of a much wilder story. Growing at a few percent a year feels normal because it's what we were born into, but in full historical context it's the part where the line goes almost straight up.
            - The first chart suggests regularity and predictability; the second suggests volatility and dramatically different possible futures. Which chart is "right" is an interpretive choice about the frame, not a disagreement about data.

- The arithmetic forcing this conclusion is simple — run 2% growth forward 8200 years and the economy ends up roughly 3 × 10^70 times its current size, which exceeds the atom count of our galaxy.
    - 2% annual growth implies a doubling about every 35 years; that compounding, extended across ~8200 years, produces the 3 × 10^70 multiple.
        - Our galaxy has fewer than 10^70 atoms, and we cannot realistically travel beyond it in that window. An economy that big in a galaxy that small would mean multiple copies of today's entire world economy per atom — clearly not how things will go.
            - 8200 years is less time than agricultural human civilization has already lasted. The current regime, only a couple of centuries old, cannot keep running for another few thousand. A parallel calculation framed in energy rather than economics hits the wall even sooner: ~2.3% annual growth in energy use would consume the full stellar output of the galaxy within about 2500 years.

- Three possible successors to this regime deserve serious attention: stagnation, explosion, and collapse.
    - Stagnation means expansion gradually slows and ends. The economy reaches whatever its physical maximum size turns out to be, and from then on the central question is how to divide a fixed pie, with the dynamic growing-pie world gone for good.
        - It seems likely we'll eventually run out of new scientific insights, technological innovations, and accessible resources, and the few-percent-a-year regime will come to an end.
            - This is the conservative reading of the math — no dramatic event, just deceleration back toward zero.

    - Explosion means growth accelerates further, potentially to extreme rates, before slamming into physical limits.
        - David Roodman of Open Philanthropy fit a curve to the long-run historical pattern, and the extrapolation implies *infinite growth* this century — which reads as: the fastest growth physically possible before hitting the ceiling.
            - The plausible mechanism is a Duplicator — a technology that effectively copies minds, with digital people or sufficiently advanced AI filling that role. With enough "minds" available, the bottleneck on output loosens dramatically.
                - In that scenario, annual growth could hit 100% (a doubling every year), which itself could only run for about 250 years before physical limits bite. Doubling every month is sustainable at most ~20 years. Faster is shorter still. The texture of that future: blindingly rapid progress, much of it produced by AIs whose output humans cannot meaningfully track, approaching the physical ceiling and then necessarily decelerating.

    - Collapse means a global catastrophe knocks civilization down to a state it never recovers from, with human extinction as the extreme version.
        - Toby Ord argues in *The Precipice* that natural risks like asteroids are unlikely to cause this.
            - The risks that do look serious and hard to quantify are all anthropogenic: climate change, nuclear war (especially nuclear winter), pandemics (especially as biology advances toward nastier bioweapons), and risks from advanced AI itself.

- The same shape appears in science and technology, even though there's no clean single chart for it — the overwhelming majority of human discovery has happened in just the last few centuries.
    - Asimov's *Chronology of Science and Discovery* is a useful data point: roughly 553 of its 654 pages cover events after the year 1500, even though the timeline begins in 4 million BC.
        - The first entries include items like stone tools, fire, religion, and art. The last pages include things like Halley's comet and warm superconductivity. The density of discovery sits wildly close to the present, and a timeline focused on any single domain — for instance, transportation and energy — would look similar.
            - Just as with economic growth, scientific and technological advancement from here can Stagnate (as some researchers now worry is already happening), Explode (if a technology dramatically multiplied the number of minds doing science — human, digital, or AI), or Collapse under catastrophe. Same three-way fork.

- Put the possibilities together and this century comes out remarkable on any reading — we live in one of the two fastest-growing centuries in all of history, almost certainly one of the ~80 fastest ever, possibly the single fastest, possibly the last.
    - The 20th and 21st centuries share the title of fastest-growing so far. Whatever happens from here, this century is highly unlikely to drop out of the top ~80 by growth rate across all of history.
        - If the right enabling technology arrives, this could be the single fastest-growing century ever, by a wide margin over everything else on the list.
            - And if things go badly enough, it could be our last. None of this analysis yet leans on detailed reasoning about AI specifically — it falls out of basic observations about growth curves and physical limits.

- The scene resembles a plane blasting down a runway while its passengers debate seatbelts — liftoff, stall, or crash is coming, and Business As Usual is not among the long-run options.
    - The plane is already moving much faster than normal, there isn't enough runway left to continue much longer, and the acceleration is still rising. Yet the conversation on board is mostly about how to arrange the seatbelt comfortably, or about how the best moments in life are watching the white lines whoosh past, or about whose fault the background roar is.
        - A sensible passenger, not knowing exactly what comes next, would at least be saying out loud: this situation is strange, unusual, and temporary; we are going to speed up further, or come to a stop, or see something else weird happen.
            - There ought to be some people thinking inside the Business As Usual headspace about how to make a steady-growth world better, and some people thinking inside the This Can't Go On headspace about what stagnation, explosion, or collapse imply and whether present actions can shift which one arrives. Right now almost all commentary sits on the Business As Usual side, and the balance is badly off.
